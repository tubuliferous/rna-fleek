▼ Navigation

3D View uses an orbit camera. You rotate around a center point marked by the navigation cross (navcross) — a small axis cross that appears while you're actively interacting, then fades after you stop. Think of it as the anchor your camera orbits around: left-drag rotates the camera while the navcross stays fixed.

The navcross has a fixed world size, so zooming (scroll) makes it grow on screen — you're moving closer to a fixed point. Flying keeps it the same apparent size because you're moving the navcross itself. This distinction helps you see whether you're changing distance or translating through the cloud.

Moving the navcross: Pan (Ctrl/⌘+drag or right-drag) slides the navcross laterally. Fly (⌥ Opt+drag or middle-drag) pushes it forward/backward along the camera's line of sight. The camera follows in both cases.

The navcross is constrained inside a bounding sphere computed from the point cloud — you can fly through the cloud but the navcross can't leave it. When you push it against the boundary, a geodesic wireframe briefly flashes to show the constraint. It fades when you stop or pull back. Zoom-out is capped at 4× the cloud radius.

On first load, the camera performs a brief intro orbit — starting close and pulling back with a half-rotation — to convey that the space is 3D and interactive. Any click or scroll cancels it.

Double-click on any cell to fly the camera to that location and zoom in to the neighborhood. The fly-to animation smoothly centers the view and zooms to 35% of the cloud radius — the same distance every time, regardless of your starting zoom level. In 3D, the navcross moves to the target cell. This is useful for quickly inspecting a region of interest spotted in the overview.

Auto-rotate: An optional slow orbit can be toggled from the View panel. When active, a small "Auto" badge appears on the button. Any manual interaction pauses auto-rotation; it resumes when you stop. This is useful for presentations or when exploring the cloud's 3D structure passively.

2D View uses an orthographic camera with pan and zoom. Panning is limited so the cloud can't be dragged fully off-screen. Zoom-out shows about 2× the cloud extent; zoom-in goes to 500×. A crosshair appears at screen center during panning to aid orientation.

Zoom acceleration: scroll speed scales with flick velocity — a gentle scroll nudges, a fast flick covers significant distance. Both 2D and 3D share the same acceleration curve.

Modifier key hints — when you hold Shift, Option, or Ctrl, the hint bar at the bottom-left updates to show what action your next drag will perform (lasso, fly, pan, etc.) before you start moving.

Shortcut flash — when you press a keyboard shortcut, a brief overlay flashes on screen showing the action name and key combination. This helps learn shortcuts and confirms the action was received. Toggle it off in Settings if you prefer a quieter interface.

The 2D/3D toggle and Reset button are always accessible in the View panel header, even when the panel is collapsed.

Sidebar: press \ or click the « button to hide the sidebar and give the visualization full width. Press \ again or click » to restore it.

▼ Embeddings

RNA-FLEEK supports multiple dimensionality reduction methods, selectable from the Embedding dropdown in the View panel. Each offers a different trade-off between local structure (how well nearby cells in the original high-dimensional space remain nearby in the plot) and global structure (how well the overall distances and relationships between cell populations are preserved).

EmbeddingLocalGlobalNotes
UMAPExcellentPoorThe standard. Tight, well-separated clusters. But distances between clusters are unreliable — related cell types may be far apart, unrelated types may be neighbors. The embedding is non-deterministic (varies between runs).
PCAPoorExcellentThe most honest view. Distances are meaningful and proportional to true expression differences. Rare populations get crushed together, and you lose resolution on subtle subtypes — but what you see is real. No hyperparameters, deterministic, instant to compute.
PaCMAPVery goodGoodExplicitly optimizes both local and global structure using a three-stage weighted loss: far pairs first (get the global layout right), then near pairs (refine clusters). Related cell types stay closer together than in UMAP, while clusters remain well-separated. Deterministic given the same seed.

Practical guidance: Start with UMAP for exploration — the clusters are easiest to see and interact with. Switch to PCA to check whether two clusters that look separate in UMAP are genuinely distant in expression space, or whether UMAP is exaggerating a small difference. Use PaCMAP when you want the best of both worlds — it's the most balanced view for understanding how cell populations relate to each other.

Important UMAP caveat: UMAP's cluster sizes, shapes, and inter-cluster distances are all unreliable. A large cluster in UMAP may be smaller than a tiny one. Two adjacent clusters may be unrelated. Two distant clusters may be almost identical. UMAP only reliably tells you that cells within a cluster are similar — nothing about the relationships between clusters.

All embeddings are available in both 2D and 3D. UMAP and PCA are computed during initial data loading. PaCMAP computes in the background after loading — the dropdown shows "PaCMAP (computing…)" until it's ready. All embeddings are cached to disk for instant reload on subsequent sessions.

Native 2D embeddings: By default, FLEEK computes only the 3D embedding and derives 2D by slicing the first two coordinates — halving computation time. Enable Native 2D embeddings in the load options to compute 2D independently, which produces tighter cluster separation in 2D views at the cost of longer load times. If a native 2D cache exists from a previous load, it is automatically preferred regardless of the current setting.

Keyboard shortcuts: Press D to toggle between 2D and 3D views. Press E to cycle through available embeddings (UMAP → PCA → PaCMAP → …).

▼ View Splits

View splits are named snapshots of your current slice filter and cluster visibility. They let you save and switch between different "angles" on the data — for example, one split filtered to organ=heart, another to organ=liver — without losing state.

Create a new view split with the + button in the Split Views panel, or press 1–9 to create/switch to "Split N" by number. You can create as many splits as you want. Switch between them using the sidebar dropdown, or press the number key matching the split name. Each split remembers its slice filter, cluster visibility, and deleted cells.

Split-screen (⧉): When you have two or more view splits, click the split-screen button to show two side by side in real time. Each half renders its own cell population based on its split's slice filter. Selection groups are shared across both halves — lasso in one half, see the highlights in both.

Reassigning halves: The split name in each half's label is a dropdown. Change it to show a different split in that half — if you pick a split already in the other half, they swap.

▼ Cell Type Annotation

RNA-FLEEK can predict cell type identities for each cluster using two complementary approaches. Both are accessed via buttons in the Cell Types panel header.

Markers (green M tag) — scores each cluster's differentially expressed genes against a curated marker gene database. For each cluster, a one-vs-rest Wilcoxon test identifies the top 50 upregulated genes. These are compared against known cell type signatures using two metrics:

MetricWhat it measures
Overlap coefficientFraction of a cell type's known markers found in the cluster's top genes. A score of 78% means 78% of that cell type's known markers were among the cluster's most distinguishing genes.
Fisher's exact testStatistical significance of the overlap — is this many shared genes more than expected by chance given the total gene universe? Low p-values mean the overlap is unlikely to be random.

The built-in database covers ~55 common human cell types (T cells, B cells, monocytes, fibroblasts, epithelial, neuronal, etc.). For broader coverage (~800+ cell types across tissues), run python download_markers.py to fetch the CellMarker2 database — the server auto-detects and uses it if present.

Why marker-based annotation works: Cell types are defined by characteristic gene expression programs. A cluster expressing CD3D, CD3E, CD8A, GZMK is almost certainly a CD8+ T cell because those genes are specifically and consistently expressed in CD8+ T cells across many studies. The overlap coefficient quantifies this match.

Limitations: Marker databases are incomplete and biased toward well-studied cell types. Novel or transitional states may not match any known signature. Small clusters (<5 cells) are skipped because they lack statistical power for DEG.

Claude (purple AI tag) — sends each cluster's top marker genes to Claude (Anthropic's LLM) for interpretation. This provides a different perspective:

AdvantageWhy
Broader knowledgeClaude has internalized marker gene associations from the full biomedical literature, not just a curated list. It can recognize rare cell types, transitional states, and tissue-specific subtypes.
ReasoningEach prediction includes a brief explanation citing specific markers, helping you evaluate the call. Hover over the AI tag to see the reasoning.
Context-awareClaude considers the gene combination holistically — it knows that CD14+LYZ+S100A8 together specifically indicate monocytes, not just that each gene is individually associated with monocytes.

Setup: Set the ANTHROPIC_API_KEY environment variable, or add it to ~/.fleek.env (recommended for shared servers). Claude annotation requires API credits (a few cents per dataset).

How it works: The server runs one-vs-rest DEG, sends the top 25 marker genes for each cluster to Claude in batches of 50 clusters. Claude returns a cell type prediction, confidence level (high/medium/low), and reasoning for each. Results are displayed as purple AI tags under each cluster name.

Using both together: The two approaches are complementary and share their most expensive computation — the one-vs-rest DEG analysis runs once and is reused by both. If you click Markers and Claude simultaneously, the DEG runs once and both methods proceed with their unique scoring steps in parallel. Marker DB annotation is instant after DEG; Claude requires API calls but shows live progress on the button ("Claude API batch 3/14… (35s)").

QuickDEG: For large datasets (100k+ cells), annotation DEG can take many minutes. The QuickDEG option (on by default) subsamples cells before running DEG — stratified by cluster to preserve proportions. With 50k cells, marker genes are virtually identical to the full dataset but computation is 10–20× faster. Quick and full DEG results are cached separately in memory — toggling QuickDEG on/off doesn't discard the other version. After both have been computed once, switching between them is instant.

Caching: Both marker and Claude annotations are cached to disk alongside the h5ad file (.annot_markers.json / .annot_llm.json). On subsequent loads, annotations appear instantly without recomputation or API calls. The shared DEG results are also cached in memory for the duration of the session. After annotation completes, the button shows a ✓ and becomes inactive — to re-run, delete the cache from the Data panel badges (hover over the badge, click ×).

▼ Developmental Lineage

The Lineage button (yellow) in the Cell Types panel constructs a developmental hierarchy of the cell types in your dataset using Claude AI. Instead of a flat list, cell types are organized into a tree showing their developmental relationships — from common progenitors down to differentiated types.

How it works: The server sends all cluster names (plus Claude cell type predictions if available) to Claude with a prompt asking it to construct a developmental lineage tree. Claude uses its knowledge of developmental biology to infer the hierarchy, including intermediate progenitor stages that may not be present in the dataset.

Node styleMeaning
Normal textCell type present in the dataset (maps to one or more clusters)
Italic, fadedInferred intermediate — a developmental stage needed to connect the lineage but not represented in the dataset

Dots use the same filled/empty convention as the flat list — filled = visible, ring = hidden. Colors match the UMAP point colors and flat list. Hover over a node to see the original cluster name from the file, cell count, and interaction hints.

Caveats:

The lineage tree is AI-generated and should be treated as an informed hypothesis, not ground truth. Key limitations:

ConsistencyClaude may produce slightly different trees for the same cell types across runs. Results are cached after the first call, so the tree is stable within a session and across reloads.
Tree simplificationReal developmental biology involves convergence, transdifferentiation, and context-dependent paths. The tree format forces a simplified view — some cell types that arise from multiple progenitors are placed in one branch.
Tissue contextThe tree is most accurate when Claude annotations have already been run, providing tissue context. Without them, the hierarchy is inferred solely from cluster names.
NamingInferred intermediate nodes use standard developmental nomenclature, which may not match dataset-specific naming conventions.

Caching: The lineage tree is cached to .annot_lineage.json. Subsequent loads skip the API call. To regenerate, delete the Lineage cache badge from the Data panel (hover, click ×) and click Lineage again.

Best practice: Clicking Lineage automatically runs Claude annotation first if it hasn't been done yet — Claude-inferred cell types are far more reliable for lineage construction than raw cluster names, which may be generic (e.g. "Cluster 0"), abbreviated, or domain-specific. If Claude annotations already exist, the lineage builds directly from them. The lineage prompt instructs Claude to use the predicted cell type names for tree nodes.

Search: The type search box works in lineage mode too — matching nodes are shown with their parent branches auto-expanded, non-matching branches are hidden. This lets you find specific cell types within the hierarchy.

▼ Gene Expression

The Gene panel is always visible in the sidebar. Switch to Gene Expression coloring with G or click the Gene Expression button. Press C to switch back to cell type coloring. Type a gene name in the search box — results come from the server's full gene list. Click a gene to load its expression on the UMAP. When the panel is collapsed, the currently selected gene name appears in the header.

Expression values are fetched on demand from the server and cached client-side. Values are normalized 0–1 using the 98th percentile of expressing cells, then mapped to a colorscale: Viridis (dark mode) or Blues sequential (light mode). The expression legend (colormap bar) appears inside the Gene panel when a gene is selected.

When Gene Expression coloring is active, additional controls appear in the View panel:

  • Non-expressing cells — toggle visibility of cells with zero detected expression. When on (default), they appear as subtle ghost dots; when off, they are hidden entirely.
  • Expression → size — scales point area proportionally to expression level. High-expressing cells appear larger; non-expressing cells shrink to tiny dots.
  • Expression glow — adds a halo glow around expressing cells, making them stand out at a distance. The glow size adapts with zoom level.
  • Pulse expressing cells — slowly pulses expressing cells and their glow brighter (towards white in dark mode, deep navy in light mode) to help spot them. The halo also breathes in size with the pulse.

A hidden cell counter appears in the bottom-left corner of the canvas when any cells are invisible (hidden clusters, slice filter, or non-expressing toggle).

Gene Variability — a ranked list of all genes shown below the search box. The label "Ranking of gene variability in:" precedes a dropdown that switches between two modes:

  • Cell Type / Cluster (default) — shows per-cluster gene rankings from the shared one-vs-rest DEG analysis (the same computation used by Claude annotation). A dropdown selects the cell type (showing Claude-inferred names when available). A colored dot to the left of the dropdown reflects that cluster's visibility — click it to toggle, Alt+click to solo, Shift+click to select into a group. The column header shows the current sort metric and clicks to cycle through: |Cohen's d| → log₂FC → −log₁₀(padj). Bars are colored by direction: accent for upregulated genes, red for downregulated. Hovering over any gene shows all three metrics in the tooltip. The DEG computation triggers automatically on first use and is cached for the session.
  • All Cells (Global) — genes ranked by variability score, measuring how much more (or less) variable each gene is than expected for its expression level. When a dataset already contains a variability metric (e.g. dispersions_norm from scanpy), that metric is used directly. When no pre-computed metric exists, FLEEK computes its own using the locally standardized trend residual method — see Methods & Statistics below for full details. HVG genes appear bold with accent-colored bars.

When to use each metric:

  • |Cohen's d| (default) — standardized effect size: the mean difference divided by the pooled standard deviation. Tells you how cleanly separated a gene's expression is between this cell type and all others. Robust to sample size — a gene with a large d is a reliable marker regardless of how many cells you have. Best for finding definitive marker genes that distinguish a cell type.
  • log₂FC — log fold change: how many doublings of expression this cell type has over the rest. Directly interpretable in biological terms (a gene at +2 is 4× higher). However, fold change can be inflated for low-expression genes where small absolute differences produce large ratios. Best for identifying strongly upregulated or downregulated genes in absolute terms.
  • −log₁₀(padj) — transformed adjusted p-value: statistical significance of the difference. Sensitive to sample size — with thousands of cells, even tiny differences reach significance. Useful for confirming a gene is reliably different (not noise), but the top-ranked genes may have trivially small effect sizes. Best used as a filter (is this real?) rather than a ranking (is this important?).

In both modes, clicking a gene loads its expression on the UMAP. Selecting a gene auto-scrolls the ranked list to that gene's position.

Red-flagged genes: Genes shown in red in the ranked list are ones that would have been excluded by traditional cutoff-based methods (scanpy's highly_variable_genes). The specific reason is shown in the hover tooltip — either low mean expression (< 0.0125), high mean expression (above the adaptive ceiling), or low detection rate (< 0.5% of cells expressing). These genes are not excluded from FLEEK's ranking — the locally standardized z-score handles them naturally. The red flag is a transparency feature: it lets you evaluate whether high-ranking red-flagged genes represent genuine biological signal that traditional methods would have missed, or artifacts that the model hasn't fully corrected for. If many red genes appear near the top, treat the rankings with extra scrutiny.

Gene index — on load, a CSC (compressed sparse column) index is built in the background for fast per-gene lookups. A spinner in the Data panel shows progress. Before the index completes, gene lookups use a slower row-scan fallback but still work. The index is cached to disk (.csc_cache.npz) — subsequent loads skip the build entirely.

View controls: Point Size, Opacity, and Unselected Brightness sliders in the View panel let you fine-tune the visualization. Point Size is normalized 1–100 (default 7%). Opacity controls global transparency of cells. Unselected Brightness fades non-selected cells toward the background — this value is maintained separately per color mode: Gene Expression mode defaults to ~2/3 brightness so expressing cells remain visible through unselected ones, while Cell Type/Cluster mode defaults to ~1/3 brightness for stronger contrast. Both values are set automatically when the first selection is made, and switching color modes swaps to the appropriate brightness. Manual slider adjustment is saved per mode.

Partial visibility indicators: Cell type dots, lineage tree dots, and slice pills all show proportional fill when some of their cells are hidden by other filters. A dot that's half-filled means roughly half of that cluster's cells are visible. Hover over any dot or pill for exact counts. A hidden-cell counter appears in the bottom-left HUD.

Selection highlight rings: scale proportionally with cell size — they get thinner when zoomed out and thicker when zoomed in, always sitting on the cell's visual circumference.

Expression rendering: In Gene Expression mode, expressing cells can optionally have their gradient edge removed for a flat disc appearance when halos are active, reducing visual noise. Non-expressing cells retain the soft gradient. This switches automatically when Expression Glow is toggled.

▼ DEG Analysis

The DEG button (enabled when 2+ groups have cells) opens the analysis panel at the bottom. Select two groups to compare, choose a test, and click Run. The Run button becomes a Stop button during computation — click to cancel.

Tests available:

TestDescription
WilcoxonRank-sum test. Non-parametric, robust for scRNA-seq. Recommended default.
t-testWelch's t-test. Faster, assumes roughly normal expression in each group.

Gene filtering: Before testing, genes expressed in fewer than 3 cells across both groups are removed. This typically reduces ~60k genes to ~10–20k, giving a 3–6× speedup without affecting results for meaningful genes.

Normalization: If the data appears to contain raw counts (integer values with max > 5 in a sample), the server auto-applies normalize_total(target_sum=1e4) and log1p to the subset before testing.

Volcano plot: log₂ fold-change on x-axis. Y-axis toggles between −log₁₀(padj) and |Cohen's d|. Dotted lines mark significance thresholds: |log₂FC| ≥ 0.5 (vertical lines) and padj < 0.05 or |d| ≥ 0.8 (horizontal line). Genes passing both thresholds are colored by group direction. A horizontal dotted line labeled "top N%" shows the visibility cutoff — genes below this line are hidden by the "Show top N%" slider. When viewing all 100% of genes, the visibility cutoff line is hidden. Default 50% shows the most significant genes, keeping the plot responsive.

Volcano navigation: Scroll to zoom both axes simultaneously. Drag the plot to pan. Drag the x-axis area (bottom) left/right to zoom just the x-axis (fold-change); drag the y-axis area (left) up/down to zoom just the y-axis (significance). Double-click to reset all axes to default view.

Interacting with results: Hover over a volcano point or table row to see gene stats and highlight the corresponding element in the other view. Click a gene (in either the volcano or the table) to load its expression on the UMAP — this lets you immediately see where a differentially expressed gene is active in the cell landscape. The gene search box in the DEG table filters results in real time. Table columns (gene, log₂FC, Cohen's d, padj) are sortable — click any header to sort.

Resizable panel: Drag the divider between the volcano plot and gene table to adjust their relative sizes. The layout is preserved during the session.

Download: Click the download button to export the full DEG table as a TSV file (tab-separated values), suitable for spreadsheets or downstream analysis tools.

Cohen's d is computed directly on the sparse matrix using E[X²]−E[X]² with Bessel correction, avoiding densification.

Caching & history: Results are cached by test type + group combination. The History dropdown lists all previous DEG analyses for the current session — click any entry to restore its results instantly, including the volcano plot and gene table. Switching between Wilcoxon and t-test shows cached results instantly if available. A "stale" badge appears on a history entry when the selection groups have changed since that analysis was run — the results are still viewable but may no longer reflect the current cell assignments.

Pseudoreplication caveat: Cells from the same donor/sample are not independent observations. P-values from single-cell comparisons are anti-conservative — they reflect intra-sample variability, not inter-donor. For publication-level results, use the pseudo-bulk mode described below.

Pseudo-bulk mode: Switch between Single-cell and Pseudo-bulk using the toggle at the top of the DEG panel. Pseudo-bulk aggregates raw counts per biological replicate within each selection group, then runs a bulk-level statistical test. This addresses the pseudoreplication problem.

Workflow:

  1. Select cells into Group A and Group B (e.g., two cell types, two conditions, or any two subsets).
  2. Open DEG panel and switch to Pseudo-bulk mode.
  3. The Group A / Group B dropdowns remain visible — select which two groups to compare.
  4. A single Replicate column dropdown appears. Select the obs column that defines biological replicates (e.g., donor, batch, sample_id). Columns with 2–50 unique values are listed.
  5. Click Run. For each group, cells are split by replicate, raw counts are summed per replicate, and the resulting pseudo-bulk count matrix is tested.

Statistical tests: pyDESeq2 is used when installed — it models count dispersion across replicates using a negative binomial GLM. If pyDESeq2 is not available, FLEEK falls back to a Welch's t-test on log₂-CPM values with Benjamini-Hochberg correction.

Requirements: A raw counts matrix must be detected in the dataset (checked automatically on load — see the Data panel). Each group needs cells in at least 2 replicates. More replicates improve statistical power. Genes with fewer than 10 total counts across all samples are filtered out.

Results: Displayed in the same volcano plot and gene table as single-cell DEG. The note above the plot shows the method used, group names, replicate counts, and number of genes tested. Cohen's d is not computed for pseudo-bulk (shown as 0).

▼ Data Management

RNA-FLEEK loads .h5ad files (AnnData format) in three ways: upload (click the upload icon or select a file), drag-and-drop (drag an .h5ad file onto the Data panel), or browse (click the folder icon to navigate the server's file system — no upload needed for local files).

File browser: Finder-style column navigation — click a directory to see its contents in a new column to the right. Swipe horizontally (trackpad or click-and-drag) to navigate up and down the hierarchy. The path bar at the top is editable — type or paste a path, press Tab for completions, Enter to navigate. Single-click a file to see load options, double-click to load immediately. Cache status dots show which files have cached embeddings, gene indices, or annotations.

Unloading: Click the power icon (⏻) in the Data panel header to unload the current dataset and free all server memory. This stops any background processing (CSC indexing, PaCMAP computation) and clears all caches.

▼ Methods & Statistics

UMAP is computed via scanpy using default parameters (n_neighbors=15, min_dist=0.3). Both 2D and 3D embeddings are computed and cached to disk. QuickMap fits on a stratified subsample (preserving cluster proportions) and projects remaining cells via batch PCA and UMAP transform. Quick and full versions are cached separately (umap_2d_quick / umap_2d_full) — switching between modes never overwrites the other. Legacy caches are accepted as fallback.

PCA — the first 2 or 3 principal components are extracted from the full PCA matrix (50 components by default). PCA is typically computed as part of the UMAP pipeline (scanpy requires it for the neighbor graph) so it adds no extra cost. The full 50-component matrix is cached and restored to adata.obsm["X_pca"] so downstream methods (PaCMAP, neighbor graph) can use it without recomputation.

PaCMAP (Pairwise Controlled Manifold Approximation) operates on the first 30 PCA components. It uses a three-stage optimization: (1) early iterations weight far pairs heavily to establish global layout, (2) mid-pairs prevent false neighbors (cells that are close in the embedding but distant in the original space), (3) late iterations refine local structure. Computed in a subprocess to avoid numba/threading conflicts with the server.

QuickMap (PaCMAP) uses subsample-and-project for datasets >50k cells. PaCMAP is fit on a stratified subsample, then remaining cells are projected via k=5 inverse-distance weighted interpolation: for each unfit cell, the 5 nearest neighbors in PCA space are found (cKDTree), and the cell is placed at the weighted average of their PaCMAP coordinates (weights ∝ 1/distance). This produces smooth interpolation — cells between clusters land between them proportionally, avoiding the discretization artifacts of simple nearest-neighbor copy. Quick and full versions are cached separately.

Leiden clustering is computed automatically when a dataset lacks cell_type or leiden annotations. Uses the igraph backend with resolution=1.0 and 2 iterations. Requires a neighbor graph — if none exists, FLEEK builds one from PCA (or computes PCA from scratch if needed). Results are cached in the unified .fleek_cache.npz file.

Differential expression uses scanpy's rank_genes_groups. P-values are adjusted for multiple testing using Benjamini-Hochberg. Genes expressed in fewer than 3 cells are filtered before testing. Clusters with fewer than 5 cells are excluded from annotation DEG. If Wilcoxon fails (e.g. edge cases with sparse data), the server falls back to t-test automatically.

DEG subsampling (QuickDEG): For large datasets, the annotation DEG is run on a stratified subsample (default 50k cells) rather than the full dataset. Each cluster contributes cells proportionally, with a minimum of 5 per cluster. The top marker genes are virtually identical whether tested on 50k or 600k+ cells — the additional cells only push p-values lower without changing rankings. This is a separate mechanism from the user-facing DEG panel (which always tests the specific selection groups on their actual cells).

Shared DEG: Both marker-based and Claude annotation share a single DEG computation. The first to run computes the one-vs-rest rankings, caches them in memory (keyed by cell count, cluster count, test method, and subsample size), and the second method gets the results instantly. Quick DEG (subsampled) and full DEG (all cells) are cached separately — switching between QuickDEG on/off doesn't invalidate the other. A threading lock ensures concurrent requests don't conflict.

Cohen's d (effect size) is computed as the difference in means divided by pooled standard deviation, with Bessel correction. Computed directly on the sparse matrix via E[X²]−E[X]² to avoid materializing dense arrays.

Marker-based annotation uses the shared one-vs-rest DEG results (top 50 genes per cluster), then scores each cluster against every cell type signature using: (1) overlap coefficient = fraction of known markers found in the cluster's top genes, and (2) Fisher's exact test for enrichment against the full gene universe. Predictions are ranked by overlap score, then p-value. The built-in database contains ~55 cell types; the full CellMarker2 database (optional download) contains ~800+.

LLM annotation uses the same shared DEG results, formatted as a structured prompt to Claude (Anthropic). The top 25 marker genes per cluster are sent in batches of 50 clusters. Claude returns cell type, confidence level, and reasoning. Each batch uses up to 8,192 output tokens. The model evaluates gene combinations holistically rather than matching individual genes against a list.

Gene variability scoring — FLEEK ranks all genes by how variable they are relative to their expression level. When a dataset already contains a variability metric (checked in order: dispersions_norm, dispersions, variances_norm, variances in adata.var), that metric is used directly. When no pre-computed metric exists, FLEEK computes its own using the locally standardized trend residual method described below.

The problem (two parts):

Part 1 — mean-variance dependence: In scRNA-seq data, variance scales with mean expression. Highly expressed housekeeping genes (ACTB, GAPDH, Malat1, ribosomal proteins) dominate raw variance rankings simply because they are highly expressed, not because they are biologically informative. A good variability score must correct for this dependence.

Part 2 — heteroscedastic scatter: Even after correcting for the mean-variance trend (e.g. via Fano factor or simple polynomial residual), the scatter around the corrected trend is not uniform across expression levels. At very low expression, residuals are noisy — Poisson counting statistics dominate, and a gene expressed in 2 out of 50,000 cells can produce a large positive residual from pure chance. At moderate expression, the same residual magnitude would be genuinely remarkable. A raw residual of +1.5 means something very different depending on expression level. Scores must account for this expression-dependent noise.

Existing approaches and their limitations:

MethodLimitation
Raw variance (σ²)Biased toward highly expressed genes — ACTB will always rank near the top regardless of its biological informativeness
Coefficient of variation (σ/μ)Biased toward barely-detected genes — a gene in 2 out of 50,000 cells gets an extreme CV from a single stochastic count
Binned z-score (scanpy dispersions_norm)Discretizes the smooth mean-variance relationship into 20 bins, then z-scores within each bin. Requires arbitrary expression cutoffs (min_mean, max_mean, min_disp) — genes outside these thresholds are silently excluded, potentially discarding real biological signal
Simple polynomial residualCorrects the mean-variance trend (solving Part 1), but does not account for heteroscedastic scatter (Part 2) — barely-detected genes with large Poisson residuals still rank at the top

FLEEK's approach: locally standardized trend residual. A two-stage method that corrects for both the mean-variance trend and the expression-dependent noise level, without arbitrary cutoffs.

Stage 1 — Trend correction:

StepOperation
1Compute per-gene mean (μ) and variance (σ²) across a random subsample of up to 50,000 cells
2Compute the Fano factor for each gene: F = σ²/μ (for genes with μ > 0)
3Transform both axes to log₁₀ space: log₁₀(μ) vs log₁₀(F)
4Fit a degree-3 polynomial to the trend: log₁₀(F̂) = a₃·log₁₀(μ)³ + a₂·log₁₀(μ)² + a₁·log₁₀(μ) + a₀
5Compute raw residual for each gene: r = log₁₀(F) − log₁₀(F̂)

Stage 2 — Local standardization:

StepOperation
6Fit a second degree-3 polynomial to |r| vs log₁₀(μ). This estimates the local scatter envelope σ̂(μ) — how much residual spread is expected at each expression level
7Compute locally standardized z-score: z = r / max(σ̂, 0.1). The floor of 0.1 prevents division by near-zero at expression levels where genes happen to sit tightly on the trend

Interpretation: The z-score is comparable across the full expression range. A z-score of +3 means the gene is 3 local standard deviations above the expected Fano factor for its expression level — regardless of whether it's a moderately expressed gene or a highly expressed one. True cell-type markers (expressed strongly in one cell type, absent in others) produce high z-scores because their excess variance is large relative to the local noise floor. Housekeeping genes score near zero because their variance falls on the trend. Critically, barely-detected genes also score near zero: their raw residuals may be large, but so is the local scatter at that expression level — the two cancel out.

Why two stages instead of one: A single polynomial fit corrects the trend (Part 1) but treats all residuals as equally meaningful. The second fit corrects the noise envelope (Part 2). Together, they produce a score where +2 means the same thing at log₁₀(μ) = −3 as it does at log₁₀(μ) = 0. This is analogous to how a z-score in statistics standardizes by both subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation — doing only the first step leaves you with residuals that are still not comparable across conditions.

Why degree 3: The mean-Fano relationship in log-log space typically shows curvature at both extremes — a cubic polynomial captures this without overfitting to local noise. This applies to both the trend fit and the scatter fit. Higher degrees risk fitting individual gene outliers; lower degrees miss the curvature and produce systematic biases at the tails.

Why no cutoffs: Every gene with detectable expression receives a z-score. There are no arbitrary thresholds on minimum mean, maximum mean, or minimum fraction of expressing cells. The local standardization naturally handles the noise at expression extremes — no gene needs to be excluded because its z-score already reflects the noise level at its expression level. This eliminates a class of silent errors where biologically relevant genes at expression extremes are dropped without the researcher's knowledge.

Cutoff transparency flags: Although FLEEK does not apply cutoffs, it computes which genes would have been excluded by traditional methods (min_mean < 0.0125, max_mean above the 95th percentile, or detection in < 0.5% of cells). These genes are flagged in red in the ranked list, with the specific reason shown on hover. This provides a built-in sanity check: if many red-flagged genes rank near the top, the local standardization may not be fully correcting for noise at that expression range, and the researcher should evaluate those genes individually. The flags are purely informational — they do not affect the scores.

FlagMeaning
Not expressedMean expression = 0 across the subsample
Low meanMean < 0.0125 (scanpy default min_mean)
High meanMean above the adaptive ceiling (max of 3.0 or 95th percentile)
Low detection rateExpressed in < 0.5% of cells

Why log-log space: The mean-variance relationship spans several orders of magnitude. Fitting in linear space would be dominated by a handful of very highly expressed genes. The log transform compresses the dynamic range, giving equal weight to genes across the full expression spectrum.

Subsample: For datasets exceeding 50,000 cells, mean and variance are computed on a random subsample (seed=42 for reproducibility). Gene-level statistics converge rapidly — the variance estimate from 50k cells is nearly identical to the full dataset for all but the rarest genes, at a fraction of the compute cost.

Comparison with pre-annotated metrics: When a dataset already has dispersions_norm (from scanpy's highly_variable_genes), FLEEK uses that directly. The locally standardized trend residual and dispersions_norm usually agree on the top ~500 genes. Differences appear (a) in the long tail, where binning artifacts produce different rankings than the smooth polynomial, and (b) at expression extremes, where cutoffs in dispersions_norm exclude genes entirely while the trend residual gives them near-zero scores. Both are valid; the trend residual avoids discretization artifacts, cutoff dependency, and heteroscedastic bias at the cost of being less established in the literature.

Expression normalization for visualization uses 98th percentile of expressing cells as the ceiling, mapping to [0, 1]. This prevents a handful of extreme outliers from washing out the color range.

Point rendering uses Three.js WebGL with circle-textured point sprites. Point size is controlled by a normalized 1–100 slider, converted to world units relative to the cloud bounding sphere (max = 1.8% of cloud radius). In 3D, sizeAttenuation provides perspective-correct depth. In 2D, world units are converted to pixels via orthographic frustum math with a 0.5× scale factor, ensuring 2D points are proportionally smaller than 3D (appropriate for the flat view where depth fill isn't needed). Point selection uses distanceToRay rather than camera distance — in dense 3D perspective scenes, this picks the point actually closest to where you're pointing. Ring highlights use a second point cloud layer with a ring texture, rendered with depth-test disabled so they're always visible.

Reset view fits the cloud precisely to the viewport in both modes. 3D computes the exact perspective distance: radius / sin(min(verticalHalfFOV, horizontalHalfFOV)) with 5% margin. 2D computes the ortho zoom level using the constraining axis. Both account for aspect ratio.

Performance: The CSC gene index takes ~5 minutes on 1.6M cells but is cached to disk for instant reload. PaCMAP runs in a subprocess to isolate numba JIT from the server's threading model. All heavy computation runs in background threads — the visualization is interactive immediately. Annotation DEG is subsampled to 50k cells by default (QuickDEG), reducing annotation time from ~10 minutes to ~30 seconds on large datasets. User-facing DEG pre-filters to expressed genes for 3–6× speedup. Volcano plot rendering uses batched Path2D operations (3 draw calls instead of thousands). All embeddings are dual-cached (quick and full versions coexist) alongside the h5ad file in a unified .fleek_cache.npz. Canvas coordinates are refreshed every frame via syncCanvasSize() to prevent selection offset drift after layout changes.
